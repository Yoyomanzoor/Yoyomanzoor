My WPM was once 90. Now it is 40 if I am lucky. Here's the story of why.

I've never been the fastest typer, but out of necessity I've gotten decently faster (coding, writing, notes that I need to finish in clinic ASAP because the next patient is already here and it's already 4:30). Recently, I've started to have wrist pain that presents later in the day and gets better with rest. The pain has gotten worse since I started wearing a watch consistently and after long typing sessions. The pain follows the median nerve territory and feels dull and sore, consistent with [repetitive strain injury](https://web.eecs.umich.edu/~cscott/rsi.html) or [carpal tunnel](https://www.uptodate.com/contents/carpal-tunnel-syndrome-clinical-manifestations-and-diagnosis). Okay, [maybe not carpal tunnel syndrome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9431262/) - I don't have abductor pollicus brevis weakness, median distribution hypesthesia or paresthesia, and my Phalen and Tinel signs are negative. So I don't need surgery, but it's still uncomfortable to type and wear my watch, so I can definitely optimize. For the watch, I stopped wearing it all the time and take it off when I'm typing to reduce stress on my wrist. For typing, well, I need to keep typing, and ideally fast, so what to do?

The first keyboard that caught my attention was the [Ergodox](https://ergodox-ez.com/). Per my cousins, they noticed a significant improvement in the typing experience on these. Plus, they look wicked cool. But, alas, as a poor grad student I couldn't possibly justify $400 on a keyboard! So I started looking for alternatives. Maybe not a fancy split keyboard, but just a regular-ish keyboard? I came across this video by [Dave2D](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DVfB_0s470I) and found his argument compelling - [otholinear keyboards](https://www.tryorthokeys.com/ultimate-guide-to-ortholinear-keyboards), which have keys linearly placed rather than staggered, are theoretically more erogonomic. Additional pros: they look cool (most important, of course), they take less desk space, they would a fun project to build, and are a gateway drug to keyboard addiction. Cons are the learning curve of a new keyboard layout and buying a prebuilt keyboard is expensive. Insofar as the learning curve, I realize I am learning new layouts all the time! For example, learning to type in Arabic very much required memorizing where keys go (especially since keyboards don't have Arabic letters on them). I also use vim, which is also basically learning a new keyboard layout. Insofar as cost, well if I make it myself it would be quite a bit cheaper.

So I found a keyboard kit on eBay for a [Planck Light](https://www.tryorthokeys.com/ultimate-guide-to-ortholinear-keyboards).
